Typos in "Inferring Phylogenies"


Six printings have now appeared. They are in two groups. Before Sinauer Associates was acquired by Oxford University Press, the name "Oxford University Press" does not appear on the back cover of the book. For those printings, to see which printing you have, look on the copyright page (the page after the title page). At the bottom is a row of digits (5 4 3 2 etc.). The last one is the number of the printing. The third printing also has a light blue background in the cover photo of the Far Flung Fir sculpture (all other editions have an olive-colored background). The first printing can also be distinguished by the absence of quotes from reviewers on the back cover, which is mostly just black. There never was a fifth printing -- it was accidentally skipped, as the printing after the 4th printing was numbered 6.

The printings that say "Oxford University Press" on the back cover are the next ones, and the numbers of the printings will be 2, 3, ... So I'll call the initial Oxford University Press printing the "second Oxford University Press printing".

What got left out?

Of course the book omits all work after early August, 2003. But some earlier works were not discussed that should have been. Particularly egregious omissions include:

  • reference to Nei and Kumar's (1999) book on molecular phylogenetics
  • reference to Paul Lewis's (2001) paper on a likelihood approach to discretely-coded morphological characters.
  • reference to Semple and Steel's (2003) book Phylogenetics
  • mention of Hartigan's (Biometrics, 1973) paper which gives the algorithm for counting number of parsimony changes in a multifurcating tree with n unordered states.
  • Fredrik Ronquist, in his review of the book in Science, points out that I did not cite two papers on speeding up parsimony algorithms by Pablo Goloboff (1996 and 1998 in Cladistics), and also a paper by Ward Wheeler (1995 in Systematic Biology) on choosing alignment parameters by optimizing taxonomic congruence.
  • An explanation of Barry and Hartigan's 1987 computation of the likelihood of a phylogeny using a general 12-parameter model, different for each branch of the tree but the same across sites, should have been provided, especially since their method is easy computationally. There was a citation to their work in Chapter 13, but the methods for the whole tree were not covered in Chapter 16 as they should have been.
  • The program MrBayes is quite widely used and cited, and should have been included in the list of "Widely used phylogeny programs and packages".
  • Ziheng Yang's 1996 paper in Journal of Molecular Evolution which gives a multi-locus likelihood method that estimates a separate rate parameter for each locus should have been covered in the section of Chapter 30 on my suggestions for a likelihood approach to the Total Evidence controversy.
  • Although I have some coverage in chapters 13 and 14 of models of molecular change that allow for nonindependence of sites in RNA and proteins, I should have returned to the subject in Chapter 29 to cover the use of structure in alignment. This is actively used with ribosomal RNAs; many protein researchers want to make use of structure, though the full integration of structure into protein alignment is difficult and here the techniques are still in their infancy.

What is incorrect?

As for incorrect arguments, there will be those who say most of the book is incorrect. Obviously I don't agree. The arguments I made that may be wrong are:

  • On page 234 in Chapter 15, where I discuss correcting for the ascertainment of varying restriction sites, I promise that I give a more general treatment of the subject in Chapter 16. But you won't find it there, as I forgot to do so. Oops. The more extensive argument I would have given is to be found in the 1992 paper of mine that was referred to on page 234.
  • The statement on page 269 that the process in the covarion states changes at a rate δ is wrong; Peter Morcos pointed out that it actually changes at a rate 2 κ δ / (1 + κ δ).
  • In addition, on page 425, at the bottom, I say that "For the moment we defer doing this until the next chapter." I deferred it even longer than that, as I never did return to the subject, even in the next chapter.
  • The "explanation" of the logic of the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) on page 316 must have made sense to me at the time I wrote it. But I can't make heads or tails of it now.
  • Some of the details of the importance sampling explanation of Griffiths and Tavaré's sampling method are incorrect, primarily the parts on page 481. They would need substantial modification of the material on that page to be correct. For a correct explanation along the same lines, see the paper by Felsenstein et al. (1999) cited on page 477 where the argument is similar, but correct. Here is a link to a free PDF of that paper. The relevant material is in section 7 of the paper.
  • The description of Ward Wheeler's 1996 method of optimization aligment on page 501 confuses it with his 1999 method of "fixed-states optimization". Optimization alignment is an approximation, but not nearly as rough a one as fixed-states optimization. (It was also reinvented, apparently independently, by Lutzoni et al. in 2000 in Systematic Biology). It delimits ambiguous regions and uses a fixed-states approach on them, but not on the whole sequence. As such it cannot be dismissed summarily as I did on page 501.
  • Daniel Huson points out that my statement on page 178 that:
    We could check whether all quartets of species satisfy the FPM condition, but this does not guarantee that there is one tree that implies all of those individual FPM conditions.
    is blatantly incorrect. If all possible FPM conditions are satisfied, it has been known at least since Buneman's work that there must be a tree that implies all of them. A better statement would have been:
    We could check whether all quartets of species satisfy the FPM condition; unless all possible quartets satisfy it, there is no guarantee that there is one tree that implies all of the individual FPM conditions.
  • Apparently I accepted a myth. In my introduction of Brownian motion on page 391, I stated that Robert Brown "observed that pollen grains in solution jiggled ceaselessly". Actually, Brian Ford, in the paper I cite there, argues that it was inclusions within the pollen grains that were observed to jiggle as a result of Brownian motion. David Wilkinson (in the letters section of the 10 March 2005 issue of Nature) seems to argue that it was small particles between the pollen grains that were seen to jiggle, but he tells me this was an editing mistake on the part of Nature. Pollen grains are apparently too big to have observable Brownian motion themselves.
  • Daniel Shriner and co-workers have pointed out that the calculations of numbers of unrooted unlabeled bifurcating tree shapes that I make in Table 3.5 are incorrect and overcount in most of the cases with an even number of tips. They note that a generating-function approach to this problem was given by Annette Dobson in 1974 (Journal of Applied Probability 11: 32-42, see pages 39-40). Dobson's numbers show mine to be incorrect. Interestingly, the method I describe in the text works -- I just didn't implement it correctly when calculating the table. The calculations for multifurcating unrooted tree shapes in that table were also wrong for even numbers of species. These errors are corrected in the table of typos above.
  • Mike Steel has pointed out that the presentation on page 271 is unclear. I should have explicitly noted that the tree T that is being evaluated is not necessarily the true tree. It will have expected pattern frequencies qi, while the true tree has expected pattern frequencies pi. Given that, the proof presented is correct.
  • In F. James Rohlf's otherwise complimentary review of the book in Journal of Classification, he points out that I misattributed the UPGMA method to Sokal and Michener's paper of 1957. Oops. It was apparently first published by Rohlf in 1962 and by Peter Sneath in the same year:
    • Rohlf, F. J. 1962. A Numerical Taxonomic Study of the Genus Aedes (Diptera: Culicidae) with Emphasis on the Congruence of Larval and Adult Classifications. Ph.D. thesis, Department of Entomology, University of Kansas.
    • Sneath, P. H. A. 1962. The construction of taxonomic groups, pp. 289-332 in Microbial Classification, eds. G. C. Ainsworth and P. H. A. Sneath. Cambridge University Press, Cambridge.
  • I found this one myself: on page 365, in the description of Swofford's paired-sites t-test approach, I note that we can test sums of differences with a test intended for means, "as these differ by a multiple of n (the number of sites), and so do their variances." That is wrong: if the variance of a single difference is σ2, then the variance of the difference of means is 1/n of this, and the variance of the difference of sums is n times this. A little consideration will show that these variances differ by a factor of n2, not n.
  • The description of Pol and Siddall's 2001 simulations on page 121 could be confusing. I say that
    In their trees the long branches are in adjacent pairs. This results in parsimony doing better than likelihood. As they lengthen the long branches, both do worse, as expected. At a certain length, parsimony falls prey to a long branch attraction effect, causing likelihood to outperform it beyond that length.
    There are really two effects of long branch attraction in their simulation, one which brings adjacent branches together, and one, which occurs more when branches are longer, which attracts more distant long branches. This is left fairly unclear by the current wording.
  • Erick Matsen has pointed out that I described the Shao and Sokal (1990) B1 tree shape statistic incorrectly on page 563. It is computed by computing for each interior node of the tree (except the root) the maximum of the number of branches between it and each tip descended from it. The reciprocals of these are then summed. For the tree in Figure 33.1 this gives 1/1 + 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/1 + 1/4 + 1/1 + 1/1 + 1/2 = 5.58333.
  • David Bryant points out that my oh-so-obvious algorithm to do UPGMA clustering in quadratic time (described on page 162) is actually wrong. If the objects getting clustered are nearest neighbors of many others, too many other distances need to be updated. (I suspect that this behavior will be rare in most data sets and that the algorithm will on average work fairly well). He notes that an elegant and simple quadratic algorithm for UPGMA was found by Fionn Murtagh:
    Murtagh, F. 1984. Complexities of hierarchic clustering algorithms: state of the art. Computational Statistics Quarterly 1: 101-113.
    for which see scans of the paper at this URL.
  • The chapter on the comparative method describes how the use of contrasts enables us to find transformed character values that are independent in their evolution. It gives the impression that it is necessary to have branch lengths vi that are the variances that are expected to accumulate in those characters. For many purposes, it is not necessary to know this much. I should have explained in that chapter that if we have branch lengths that are proportional to the variances, we use those and the resulting transformations still achieve independence and a common distribution, and allow us to test covariation (correlation) among the characters.
  • Andrew Roger points out that my equating clocklike trees with ultrametric trees on page 161 is not quite right. A tree could satisfy the ultrametric conditions, in that there is a point in the tree equidistant from all tips, but if it were not rooted at that point, it would not be clocklike. Thus the two are not quite the same. Also, if the rate of evolution speeds up (or slows down) by the same amount in all lineages, then the resulting tree will be ultrametric but not clocklike.
  • In the material in Chapter 16 on Hidden Markov Models for rate variation along molecules, I explain the workings of the Backwards Algorithm for summing up the likelihood in an efficient way. It's fine except for the name I give it. The algorithm I explain is technically the Forwards Algorithm, not the Backwards Algorithm. The name is puzzling because it is working its way backwards, not forwards, along the molecule. (The names Forwards and Backwards are not good ones because the way they differ is independent of which direction they are used in -- in this case perhaps we should have called it the Forwards-Algorithm-Going-Backwards).
  • Shlomo Moran points out that my statement on page 212 about the LogDet distances for two successive branches (with an infinitely long sequence) not adding up unless the intermediate base composition is (1/4, 1/4, 1/4, 1/4) is wrong. Formulas 13.29 through 13.32 can be used to show that they do add up properly, and the results by Steel and Lockhart cited on the next page, they are "tree additive" necessarily imply that as well. However it is still true, as in the numerical example given in the top part of page 212, that the distances overestimate the true branch lengths, even though they are tree-additive.
  • In Chapter 27 I show a likelihood curve for estimation of Θ in Figure 27.2. I say that it is from a simulated dataset with 50 tips and 500 sites. I have now found the output file for that run (alas not yet the data set itself) and find that the data set had 50 tips but only 200 sites.
  • In my discussion of restriction site models I discussed AFLP data, and claimed that AFLP data would be similar to restriction fragment data in which the length of the restriction site would be the sum of the lengths of the recognition sequences of the two restriction sites used, plus the lengths of the two primers used. Liz Parks of North Carolina State University points out that the lengths of the two primers should not be included because they are molecules that are ligated on, and they are not previously present in the organism and thus cannot mutate.
  • Peter Morcos points out that the Kronecker product structure of the covariance matrix on page 420 would be clearer and more consistent with the previous useage (pages 402-403) if the order of the matrices T and A were reversed. It can be done either way but his way is better.
  • On page 495, equation 28.6, the likelihood for multiple coalescent trees in a species tree, is presented as if I derived it. It was actually presented by Wayne Maddison (1997) and I should have cited him for that.
  • Here's an embarrassing one. In Chapter 10, on page 132, I show the phylogeny that was published in Fitch and Margoliash's 1967 paper. In the caption I say that it was produced from distances based on cytochrome sequences. I also carefully include in the Fitch-Margoliash figure its original caption from their paper. But apparently I did not actually read their caption closely. It clearly says that the distances came from globin sequences!
  • On page 29, in the discussion leading to equation 3.2, I consider the tree shapes ordered so that we consider the basal split of the tree into subtrees whose sizes are 1:5, 2:4, then 3:3. In the discussion after the equation, and in the figure showing unrooted trees, they are presented in the order 5:1, 4:2, then 3:3. This of course is inconsistent. For clarity, one ordering or the other should have been adopted. It does not change the count of the numbers of tree topologies as one could do that either way.

What about a second edition?

There has been five further printings, and there will be additional reprintings as necessary, with correction of typos. I don't think there is ever likely to be a full second edition. Work on phylogenies is growing rapidly, and the second edition would have to be twice the size. Aside from whether people would buy or read such a massive tome, there is the issue of who has the energy to write it. I don't. Follow-up books probably either will have to be more narrowly focussed on particular subfields, or will skim more lightly through the literature, citing much less of it.
